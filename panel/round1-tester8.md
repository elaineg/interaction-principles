# Rob — Brand/visual designer

**Persona:** Freelance brand/visual designer. Lives in Figma, Illustrator, Photoshop, Drive, client Slacks. Does static asset-prep grunt work weekly. Price-sensitive, compares everything to "I could do this in Photoshop in 4 minutes." Curious about motion because clients increasingly ask for "a bit of motion," but it's not his core.

## Clarity — Yes
Within 5 seconds I knew exactly what this is. Header "INTERACTION PRINCIPLES / interactive lessons in how interfaces feel," "08 LESSONS," a numbered lesson rail down the left, and a giant "content moves with your finger." with a DRAG ME box. I'd tell a friend: "It's a free interactive course teaching how UI motion should *feel* — you drag stuff and tweak sliders to see why good animation works." The plain-English blurb and the live demo together make it obvious. Nothing confused me. Genuinely clean editorial design — as a designer I respect the typography and restraint.

## Value — No (for me specifically)
The build quality is excellent and the demos actually work — at 2× gain the box outran my cursor, status flipped to "degraded / feels slippery," delta jumped to 242px. That's a smart way to *feel* a concept. Lesson 2's live spring-vs-easing with mass/stiffness/damping vs duration+cubic-bezier is legit.

But this is teaching me to *build motion engines* — spring integrators, frame budgets, JND, velocity handoff. That's interaction-*engineering* knowledge. My weekly grunt work is exporting assets, prepping logos, color-correcting, batch-resizing in Photoshop. None of that is here, and nothing here saves me time on it. When a client asks for "a bit of motion" I open Figma's prototype tab or hand it off — I don't hand-tune a damping coefficient. The "why it matters for your portfolio" notes assume a product/UX-motion portfolio, not a brand portfolio. So honestly: cool for 10 minutes of curiosity, zero recurrence for me. I'd open it once and never return.

## Advocacy — 5/10
Not because it's bad — it's the most polished thing on this panel I'd guess. It's a 5 because it's aimed past me. I would NOT bring it up unprompted to my brand-designer peers; they'd react like I did (nice, not for us). I *would* forward it to the one ex-colleague who went into product/UX motion design — for that person it's a 9. The thing holding it back from a broad recommend is audience: it's a course for people who *engineer* interaction, and the portfolio framing makes that explicit ("Building both engines from scratch... Show both in your portfolio"). For a visual/brand freelancer it's edutainment, not a tool.
