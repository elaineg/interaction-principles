# Dana — Demand-gen marketer

**Tech: medium · MacBook in cafes, phone between meetings · HubSpot/GA4/Notion/Canva/LinkedIn/Google Ads**

## Clarity — Yes
Within 5 seconds I knew what it is. The header "INTERACTION PRINCIPLES / interactive lessons in
how interfaces feel," the "08 LESSONS" badge, and the big "content moves with your finger." headline
plus a drag-me box told me instantly: this teaches how UI motion/interaction should feel, with live
demos. The left-rail lesson list (direct manipulation, springs vs easing, animation principles…) made
the scope obvious. No hunting. Clean, confident, editorial. I'd explain it to a friend as: "a free
interactive course that teaches designers/devs why good UI feels right — you drag sliders and feel the
difference yourself."

## Value — No (for me)
This is well-built and the demos actually work — I dragged the box, flipped GAIN to 2×, pushed latency
to 100ms and watched the status row update; in lesson 4 the toggles for anticipation/follow-through/
squash-and-stretch are genuinely satisfying. But it's not for me. The "Why it matters for your portfolio…
showing a recruiter you obsess over the thing under the finger" note tells me exactly who it's for:
people who BUILD interactions and are job-hunting. I'm a demand-gen marketer. I live in HubSpot, GA4,
Canva, and LinkedIn Campaign Manager. I brief designers; I don't author motion. My faint stake ("why
does this animation feel cheap on our landing page") is real, but this is an 8-lesson curriculum on
spring physics, frame budgets, and JND — way more than I'd ever invest to write a one-line designer
brief. Today I solve that by saying "make it feel snappier" in a Notion brief and trusting the designer.
This doesn't save ME time; it'd save the person I'm briefing time. Nothing here plugs into my workflow,
nothing exports, nothing I'd reuse weekly.

## Advocacy — 4/10
Not because it's bad — it's clearly good. The 4 reflects MY mouth, not the product's quality. I would
not bring this up unprompted to my marketing peers; none of them build motion either. The one realistic
move: I'd screenshot it and drop it in our designer's Slack DM with "you'd probably like this" — that's
maybe a 5 of usefulness to them, but for me-recommending-to-my-network it's a 4. What holds it back for
my world: zero marketer hook (no "make your landing page convert / feel premium" angle), it's a learning
commitment not a tool, and the portfolio/recruiter framing makes it explicitly an IC-designer artifact.
If it had a 60-second "spot the cheap animation" diagnostic I could run on my own landing page, I'd care.
